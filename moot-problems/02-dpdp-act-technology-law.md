markdown# Moot Court Problem 02 — Technology Law
## Riya Singh v. DataSphere Technologies Pvt. Ltd. & Union of India
**Statute: Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023**
*Difficulty: Advanced | Suitable for: BA LLB Year 4-5, LLB Year 2-3*

---

## Background Note for Participants

This problem is set in the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court of India
under Article 32 of the Constitution. Participants should research the
Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023, the Information Technology
Act 2000, and constitutional jurisprudence on the right to privacy,
equality, and the right to be forgotten.

---

## Fact Pattern

DataSphere Technologies Pvt. Ltd. (Respondent No. 1) is an AI-driven
recruitment platform operating across India. Its proprietary algorithm —
"HireIQ" — analyses candidate profiles, academic records, social media
activity, and inferred behavioural patterns to generate employability
scores for corporate clients.

Riya Singh (Petitioner), a postgraduate law student aged 24, applied
for a position at a leading law firm through the DataSphere platform.
Her application was automatically rejected by HireIQ without human
review. Upon requesting the basis for rejection under Section 8 of the
Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023, she was informed only that
she had received a score of 34/100 with no further explanation.

Investigation revealed that HireIQ had accessed her social media posts
from 2019–2021 — including posts about mental health awareness — and
had assigned a negative weighting to applicants associated with mental
health content. The algorithm had never been audited for discriminatory
outcomes. DataSphere claims its algorithm is a trade secret and refuses
to disclose its methodology.

The Petitioner filed a writ petition before the Supreme Court challenging:
(a) DataSphere's refusal to explain the automated decision; (b) the
processing of her sensitive personal data without specific consent;
and (c) the constitutional validity of Section 17 of the DPDP Act 2023
insofar as it exempts government entities from data protection obligations.

---

## Issues for Argument

**Issue 1 — Right to explanation for automated decisions**
Whether the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 confers a right
on data principals to receive an explanation of automated decisions
made about them, and if the Act is silent, whether such a right arises
from Article 21 of the Constitution as interpreted in *K.S. Puttaswamy*.

**Issue 2 — Sensitive personal data and consent**
Whether the processing of social media posts referencing mental health
constitutes processing of sensitive personal data requiring specific
consent under the DPDP Act 2023, and whether DataSphere's general
terms of service constitute valid consent within the meaning of
Section 6 of the Act.

**Issue 3 — Constitutional validity of Section 17 exemptions**
Whether Section 17 of the DPDP Act 2023, which grants the Central
Government broad exemption from data protection obligations on grounds
of national security and public order, satisfies the proportionality
standard established in *K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India* (2017).
