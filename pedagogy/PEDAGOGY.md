## Teaching Philosophy

Legal education must cultivate both analytical rigour and the courage
to argue. Moot court training is not a peripheral activity — it is the
closest the law school curriculum comes to the actual practice of law.
A student who can identify the strongest argument on both sides of a
legal question, construct it from first principles, and defend it under
questioning from a bench has developed the core professional competency
that the Bar Council of India describes as the purpose of legal education.

This framework is grounded in two pedagogical methods: the **case method**
and the **Socratic seminar**. The case method requires students to extract
legal principles from judicial decisions rather than receiving them as
propositions. The Socratic seminar requires students to defend their
extracted principles against targeted questioning. Together, they prepare
students for the intellectual demands of moot court advocacy and,
ultimately, professional legal practice.

This framework aligns with the competency outcomes specified in the
National Education Policy 2020 (critical thinking, communication,
problem-solving, and collaborative inquiry) and with the Bar Council
of India's standards for practical legal training.

## 4-Week Moot Court Training Schedule

### Week 1 — Foundation: Reading Law and Identifying Arguments

**Objective**: Students can read a judicial decision, identify the ratio
decidendi, and distinguish it from obiter dicta.

| Day | Activity | Duration |
|-----|----------|----------|
| 1 | Introduction to moot court format, roles, and court etiquette | 1 hour |
| 2 | Case reading workshop — *Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India* | 2 hours |
| 3 | Identifying ratio and obiter — small group exercise | 1.5 hours |
| 4 | Constructing a legal argument from a single case — written exercise | 1 hour |
| 5 | Peer review of written arguments + faculty feedback | 1.5 hours |

**Assessment**: one-page written argument identifying the strongest
precedent for a given legal proposition.

---

### Week 2 — Development: Research, Authorities, and Counter-Arguments

**Objective**: Students can conduct structured legal research, build
an authorities bundle, and anticipate the opposing case.

| Day | Activity | Duration |
|-----|----------|----------|
| 1 | Legal research methodology — databases, citation, verification | 1.5 hours |
| 2 | Assign Problem 01 (BNS 2023) — individual research phase | Self-directed |
| 3 | Argument mapping workshop — for and against on each issue | 2 hours |
| 4 | Introduce bench memo concept — how judges evaluate arguments | 1 hour |
| 5 | First practice oral round — 5 minutes per speaker, no interruption | 2 hours |

**Assessment**: oral practice round assessed on argument structure only
(not delivery). Feedback focused exclusively on whether the law is
correctly stated and applied.

---

### Week 3 — Refinement: Bench Interaction and Under-Pressure Advocacy

**Objective**: Students can maintain and adapt their argument under
sustained bench questioning.

| Day | Activity | Duration |
|-----|----------|----------|
| 1 | Bench questioning techniques — types of questions and how to respond | 1 hour |
| 2 | Socratic seminar — faculty plays hostile bench on Problem 01 | 2 hours |
| 3 | Switch sides exercise — students argue the opposite position | 1.5 hours |
| 4 | Introduce Problem 02 (DPDP Act) — research and preparation | Self-directed |
| 5 | Practice round with full bench — 8 minutes per speaker | 2 hours |

**Assessment**: practice round assessed using full scoring rubric
(see below). Written self-assessment required after the round.

---

### Week 4 — Mastery: Full Simulation and Assessment

**Objective**: Students demonstrate professional-standard moot court
advocacy across a full simulated competition.

| Day | Activity | Duration |
|-----|----------|----------|
| 1 | Memorial writing workshop — structure, citation, word limits | 2 hours |
| 2 | Memorial submission deadline — Problem 03 (Constitutional Law) | — |
| 3 | Full moot simulation — Preliminary Round | 3 hours |
| 4 | Bench feedback session — detailed individual feedback | 1.5 hours |
| 5 | Final round + prize distribution + debrief | 2.5 hours |

**Assessment**: full scoring rubric applied. Memorials assessed
separately (40% of total score).

## Scoring Rubric — Oral Advocacy

*Aligned with Bar Council of India practical training assessment norms*

| Criterion | Descriptor | Marks |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Knowledge of law** | Accurate statement of legal principles; correct citation of authorities; awareness of conflicting precedents | /25 |
| **Application to facts** | Ability to apply legal principles precisely to the given fact pattern; avoidance of irrelevant law | /25 |
| **Quality of argument** | Logical structure; persuasive force; ability to distinguish adverse cases; originality of argument | /20 |
| **Bench interaction** | Composure under questioning; ability to answer questions directly; returning to the argument after interruption | /20 |
| **Court etiquette** | Appropriate address of bench; correct citation practice; time management; professional demeanour | /10 |
| **Total** | | **/100** |

### Grade Descriptors

| Marks | Grade | Descriptor |
|-------|-------|------------|
| 85–100 | Outstanding | Advocates at a standard consistent with junior professional practice |
| 70–84 | Distinction | Demonstrates command of law and confident bench interaction |
| 55–69 | Merit | Solid legal knowledge with developing advocacy skills |
| 40–54 | Pass | Basic competency demonstrated; significant development needed |
| Below 40 | Refer | Does not yet demonstrate minimum moot court competency |

## BCI and NEP 2020 Alignment

This framework aligns with the following regulatory and policy mandates:

**Bar Council of India Rules on Legal Education, Rule 25**: requires
law schools to provide practical training including moot courts, client
interviewing, and legal aid. This framework satisfies Rule 25's moot
court requirement with a structured, assessable programme.

**National Education Policy 2020**: identifies critical thinking,
communication, and problem-solving as core graduate competencies.
The 4-week schedule builds each competency progressively — from reading
(critical thinking) through research (problem-solving) to oral advocacy
(communication).

**NAAC Assessment Criterion 2 (Teaching-Learning and Evaluation)**:
the scoring rubric and structured feedback mechanism satisfy NAAC's
requirement for transparent, criterion-referenced assessment in
practical legal education.
