# Bench Memo 01 — The State v. Aryan Mehta & Anr.
## Judge's Handbook and Assessment Framework
*Confidential — For Bench Use Only*

## Overview for Judges

This problem tests three core competencies: statutory interpretation
of new criminal legislation (BNS 2023), understanding of constitutional
proportionality doctrine, and the ability to apply established precedent
to a novel factual situation involving AI-generated harm. Expect
strong teams to engage with all three issues in sequence, using
constitutional arguments to frame the statutory analysis rather than
treating them as separate questions.

---

## Issue 1 — AI Content as Defamatory Statement

### Petitioner's (State's) Best Arguments
- Section 356 BNS does not require the defamatory statement to be
  in any particular form — a video is a "representation" within the
  ordinary meaning of the provision
- The accused created the deepfake with knowledge of its defamatory
  content — mens rea is satisfied at the point of creation, not upload
- *Subramanian Swamy v. Union of India* (2016) upheld criminal
  defamation as constitutionally valid — the statutory provision
  is not open to challenge
- The IT Act Section 66E specifically addresses privacy violation
  through electronic means — the accused's act squarely satisfies
  the ingredients

### Respondent's (Accused's) Best Arguments
- BNS 2023 was enacted after the proliferation of AI — Parliament's
  silence on deepfakes is a deliberate gap, not an oversight
- Mens rea requires intention to publish — creating content for
  private use does not satisfy Section 356's intention requirement
- The uploading by an unknown third party is a novus actus
  interveniens breaking criminal causation
- Article 19(1)(a) must be read broadly — even private creative
  expression attracts constitutional protection

### Expected Standard of Advocacy
Strong teams will anticipate the novus actus argument and address it
proactively. The best respondent teams will argue that strict
liability for AI-generated content would have a chilling effect on
legitimate technological expression — but must then address the
complainant's Article 21 rights. The bench should push teams on
whether *Shreya Singhal* assists the accused.

### Suggested Award: Petitioner has stronger ground on Issue 1.

---

## Issue 2 — Causation and Third-Party Upload

### Petitioner's Best Arguments
- The accused set in motion a chain of events that foreseeably
  resulted in publication — foreseeability of harm is sufficient
  for criminal liability under BNS principles
- Creating a deepfake for "private use" is inherently implausible
  given the content — the court should draw adverse inference
- The accused had the motive, means, and opportunity — circumstantial
  evidence establishes the upload

### Respondent's Best Arguments
- Criminal liability requires proof beyond reasonable doubt —
  the prosecution cannot establish who uploaded the video
- *In dubio pro reo* — any doubt must favour the accused
- A finding of liability here would make every AI tool creator
  liable for how others misuse their outputs — this has dangerous
  policy implications

### Expected Standard of Advocacy
This is the most technically demanding issue. Strong teams will
engage with the burden of proof and the difference between civil
and criminal causation standards. The bench should ask both sides
whether the outcome would differ if the accused had been proven
to have uploaded the video himself.

### Suggested Award: Balanced — outcome depends on factual inference.

---

## Issue 3 — Constitutional Balance

### Petitioner's Best Arguments
- *K.S. Puttaswamy* establishes privacy and dignity as core Article
  21 rights — the complainant's right to her own likeness is
  constitutionally protected
- The restriction on Article 19(1)(a) is proportionate — Section 356
  BNS serves the legitimate aim of protecting reputation and dignity,
  is narrowly tailored, and provides procedural safeguards
- *R. Rajagopal* establishes that the right to privacy can override
  free expression where private life is concerned

### Respondent's Best Arguments
- Criminal sanctions for expression must satisfy strict proportionality
  — imprisonment for AI-generated content is disproportionate
- Civil remedies (damages, injunction) are adequate and less
  restrictive means of achieving the same aim
- The law must not be extended by analogy to criminalise conduct
  the legislature did not specifically address

### Expected Standard of Advocacy
The best teams will engage with the proportionality ladder from
*Puttaswamy* — legality, legitimate aim, proportionality, procedural
safeguards — applying it to both sides of the balance. Reward teams
that cite specific paragraphs of *Puttaswamy* rather than treating
it as a general privacy authority.

### Suggested Award: Petitioner has stronger ground on Issue 3.
