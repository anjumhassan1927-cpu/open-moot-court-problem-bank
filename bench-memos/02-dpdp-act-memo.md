# Bench Memo 02 — Riya Singh v. DataSphere Technologies Pvt. Ltd.
## Judge's Handbook and Assessment Framework
*Confidential — For Bench Use Only*

## Overview for Judges

This is a constitutional and statutory law problem of advanced
difficulty. It tests participants' ability to apply privacy
jurisprudence to a new statutory framework (DPDP Act 2023), construct
arguments from legislative silence, and engage with comparative law
(EU AI Act) as a persuasive authority. Expect the strongest teams to
use EU AI Act provisions as comparative tools while arguing from
Indian constitutional first principles.

---

## Issue 1 — Right to Explanation for Automated Decisions

### Petitioner's Best Arguments
- Article 21 post-*Puttaswamy* includes the right to informational
  self-determination — a person has a constitutional right to know
  the basis of decisions made about them using their own data
- *Maneka Gandhi* establishes that any procedure affecting life and
  liberty must be fair, just, and reasonable — an algorithmic black
  box fails this standard
- The EU AI Act 2024 (Article 13) requires transparency for
  high-risk AI in employment — this is the emerging global standard
  and Indian courts should not lag behind it
- Section 8 of the DPDP Act creates a duty of accuracy — this
  implicitly requires the data fiduciary to be able to explain
  how data was used to reach conclusions about the data principal

### Respondent's Best Arguments
- The DPDP Act 2023 does not expressly create a right to explanation
  — courts cannot read in rights the legislature chose not to enact
- Trade secret protection is a legitimate competing interest —
  compelling disclosure of proprietary algorithms would violate
  DataSphere's Article 19(1)(g) right to carry on business
- The EU AI Act is a foreign instrument with no binding authority
  in Indian courts — it cannot fill gaps in Indian statute

### Expected Standard of Advocacy
This is the central issue and the most open-ended. The best teams
will argue that constitutional rights do not require explicit
statutory recognition — they arise from the Constitution itself.
Push petitioner teams on whether the right to explanation is
absolute or can be limited by trade secret protection.

### Suggested Award: Petitioner has stronger constitutional ground.

## Issue 2 — Sensitive Personal Data and Consent

### Petitioner's Best Arguments
- Mental health is inherently sensitive personal data — processing
  it requires specific, informed, and express consent under the
  DPDP Act's consent framework
- A general terms of service agreement does not constitute the
  specific consent required for sensitive data processing —
  *bundled consent* has been rejected globally and should be
  rejected here
- Section 6 requires consent to be for a specific purpose —
  analysing social media posts from 2019 for employment purposes
  in 2025 is outside any purpose the petitioner consented to

### Respondent's Best Arguments
- Social media posts are public — the petitioner voluntarily
  published the content and cannot now claim a privacy right
  in it
- The DPDP Act does not create a separate category of sensitive
  personal data with higher protection — this was a deliberate
  legislative choice distinct from the Srikrishna Committee draft
- DataSphere relied on publicly available information — it did
  not obtain data through breach or deception

### Expected Standard of Advocacy
Strong petitioner teams will argue the contextual integrity
principle — data shared in a personal context (mental health
advocacy) retains its sensitive character when reprocessed in
an employment context. Strong respondent teams will argue that
the legislature's omission of a sensitive data category was
deliberate and the court cannot supply it.

### Suggested Award: Balanced — strongest legal arguments favour
petitioner on consent; respondent has a tenable position on the
absence of a sensitive data category.

## Issue 3 — Constitutional Validity of Section 17

### Petitioner's Best Arguments
- Section 17 grants the government sweeping exemptions from data
  protection obligations with no defined limits — this fails
  the *Puttaswamy* proportionality test at the first stage
  (legality/defined limits)
- Blanket exemptions for national security without judicial
  oversight are constitutionally impermissible post-*Puttaswamy*
- The government exemption enables state AI surveillance without
  any data protection safeguard — this is incompatible with
  the fundamental right to privacy

### Respondent's Best Arguments
- Parliament is entitled to balance privacy against national
  security — courts must defer to legislative judgment on
  national security matters (*Ram Jethmalani v. Union of India*)
- Section 17 is a standard exemption found in every data
  protection regime globally — it does not render the entire
  Act unconstitutional
- The petitioner's case does not involve government data
  processing — this issue is not live on the facts and the
  court should decline to decide it

### Expected Standard of Advocacy
This is the most constitutional issue. The best teams will
argue that *Puttaswamy* changed the framework for security
exemptions — the government can no longer claim unlimited
national security discretion without satisfying proportionality.
Respondent teams should argue justiciability — whether this
court should decide the constitutional question on these facts.

### Suggested Award: Petitioner has the stronger constitutional
argument; Section 17 is difficult to defend under *Puttaswamy*
proportionality.
